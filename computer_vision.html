Automated Door Opening Arm

   With the William E. Carter School located in Boston Massachusetts as a client, I am working with a team of four to develop a door opening mechanism as a senior design project. By researching and applying Computer Vision, we want to enable those confined to a wheelchair to open a door with minimal user input and in keeping with the Carter School’s mission statement, maximize personal independence.




Procedure overview

  The system will detect the location of a door handle with a computer vision component, unlatch the handle via arobotic arm, and then open the door the linear actuators.


Above is a brief outline of the door opening process.

  1. Users button press will send a wakeup signal to the microcontroller
    1. We will be using a Beaglebone Black programed primarily in C++
  2. The camera will be activated and exit its lower power mode
  3. The camera will acquire pixel data
    1. While a Kinect will be utilized for the final product, for Computer Vision tests we can utilize a generic laptop camera
  4. Once the raw data is send back to the microprocessor, the camera will enter its low power mode
  5. Positional data on the door handle will be extracted and sent the robotic arm
    1. As of December 2016, we can identify two common types of door handles: door levers and door knobs.
  6. The arm will attempt to unlatch the door handle
  7. Sensors on the arm will be used to confirm interact with the door handle
  8. Feedback will be sent to the user via colored LED to confirm success or failure
    1. Multiple colors may be used to indicate specific issues: door handle undetected, distance too far, low power, ect
  9. Should all condition we met, the linear actuators will physically push/pull open the door




Block Diagrams

   Above is a brief outline of the two main components. (1) A door handle Detection Component will receive data from a camera and will primarily generate coordinate data from the pixel data. (2) The Door Opening Component will receive the coordinate data and open the door.

   My primary responsibilities focus on the development of the detection component. This can be broken down into two smaller steps. The initial step utilizes Computer Vision to identify the location of the door handle object within the pixel data and filter out errors based on number of objects. The secondary utilizes the pixel’s identified to find x,y,z positional data and filter out errors based on distance.





Results

   As of December 2016, the primary aspects of the Computer Vision step are complete. As seen below, the approximate pixel region of door handle objects can be identified. The test code utilizes a haar object classifier trained from approximately 800 positive and 900 negative sample images. The demo code exists on my github.

Future improvements:
   To improve the usability of the classifier, the variety and number of sample images used in training will be increased. In addition the sampling of multiple images per second will allow samples with multiple/no objects detected to be filtered out without major losses in detection time efficiently.

   In terms of coordinate generation, the primary focus has been on depth aspect. Utilizing the Kinect, we were able to identify depth. In the above image, the outline of a door handle was isolated based on depth position.

Future improvements:
   This step will be improved upon in the future to incorporate positional data outputs that can be utilized by the arm.





Robotic Arm

   A robotic arm with 7 degrees of freedom was choosen. It is currently controlled by an Arduino Uno to test its stability and precision.
