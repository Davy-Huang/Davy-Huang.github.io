BE 428


The Project:

Affordable screening of eye problem in LRS



   Our goal is to develop a low cost diagnostic method to screen human patients for simple eye issues in low resources environments.



Post VII

26 March 2017

Written by Toby



Latest update on Language of Design (visual instructions):

After developing different designs last week, we had to decide which design of the Mobile Lense Changer we want to follow in the next weeks. We discussed the advantages and disadvantages of each design intensively by making a Pugh Chart (unfortunately we’ve directly removed the sketching without taking a photo). In addition to the criteria from the first Pugh Chart (see blog post 3), we added ‘local resource’ and ‘easy to clean’ which came up during our interviews. After conducting the analysis, it became clear that a modified version of the P-design fulfils the needs for a Mobile Lense Changer the most. As the end-user will not produce or assemble the Mobile Lense Changer (besides changing the lenses), we decided to combine the language of design and the sequence design.

Instructions included with product:

Our goal is that we want to create a visual language by using images and line drawing. As our target market are LRS in Africa, this is even more important. First, many people do not know how to read and secondly, there is a great variety of languages and dialects and it would be a lot of work to cover all. Because of this, the sequence design and language of design are the only instructions included with the product.

Discussion of plan to incorporate sensors and actuators:

We discussed if we want to use incorporate sensors or actuators in our device and we decided that in our case this does not make sense.

Updates on interviews:

On March 23th 2017, we conducted an interview with a BU student who works as an assistant in an optometrist clinic since 2013. As we have already decided what our first prototype should look like, we were excited to get to know his point of view about it based on his experience in the optometrist clinic. We learned that an appropriate range between the different prescriptions is 0.5 diopters and that the majority of visual defects varies between -4.0 diopters and +3.0 diopters. Furthermore, he told us that the cylinder varies only a little and he recommended us to ignore this variation. By getting this important information, we were able to conclude that our Mobile Lense Changer is going to need 15 different lenses. Furthermore, we gain insights into the donation process of glasses and he gave us recommendations how we get access to old lenses.

Next steps:

Post VI

12 March 2017

Written by Anna



Sterilization and Cleaning Procedures:

There is no anticipated sterilization method that will be required for our device since it will not be in contact with the body and we want the product to require minimal cost and resources. Cleaning procedures include using water or rubbing alcohol to wipe down the device between patient uses.

Initial Instruction and Language of Design:

The goal of this device is to allow health care worker that are not trained optometrists to conduct eye screenings. The device will allow health care workers to get a general idea of their patient’s necessary eye glasses prescription. The purpose of this is 1) to provide a quick, easy way of initial eye screening, 2) to allow for first steps of obtaining eye glasses by giving a general prescription, 3) to decrease social stigmas that exist with wearing eye glasses by allowing patients to see the benefits of corrected vision, and 4) make use of damaged donated eye glasses and be able to appropriately distribute ones in good shape.

This device requires to work with organizations that donate glasses to sub-Saharan Africa. The sphere value of the prescription will be aspect of the lenses that the device is categorized by. This represents the value that corresponds to whether a patient is near-sighted or far-sighted and what strength o¬f lens is required to correct focus. Once the patient has this value, they will be able to have an estimate of their needed prescription and take further steps to obtain glasses.

1. Lenses organization
 a. Obtain donated glasses.
 b. Remove lenses from 12 pairs of glasses that correspond to the following sphere prescription levels:
  i. 5.75 to 6.50
  ii. 4.75 to 5.50
  iii. 3.75 to 4.50
  iv. 2.75 to 3.50
  v. 1.75 to 2.50
  vi. 0.75 to 1.50
  vii. -5.75 to -6.50
  viii. -4.75 to -5.50
  ix. -3.75 to -4.50
  x. -2.75 to -3.50
  xi. -1.75 to -2.50
  xii. -0.75 to -1.50
 c. Clean lenses with rubbing alcohol
 d. Add adhesive tab to lens
 e. Use tab to insert lens into corresponding spot labeled on the device
2. Eye screening
 a. Grab one lens by tab and insert into the appropriate slot on the top of the device
 b. Put device over one side patient’s face with the lens over one eye. Have the patient close the other eye
 c. Ask it the lens improves the patient’s general visual screening
 d. Take note of patient’s response and len’s number
 e. Repeat steps a-d until the most appropriate lens for the patient is determined
 f. Repeat a-e for the other eye
3. Post screening
 a. Record the most appropriate lens for the patient
 b. Inform patient that the number represents a general zone of their prescription
 c. Compare donation glasses within the recorded range for the patient or refer patient to place where glasses can be obtained

Written by Kevin



Overview:

This week our project group had 3 interviews which give us valuable insight on the direction we must take our project in. Following these interviews and the meetings from last week we have decided on a solution and are now deciding how we are going to approach our solution. We have decided on how to create our prototype and how we will produce a look-alike model.

Interviews:

Interview 1: Genna and Jeremy Mvalo

In this meeting we interviewed a Peace Corp volunteer (Genna Mvalo) and a Malawian (Jeremy Mvalo). Starting with the interview with Jeremy Mvalo, we learned some very interesting facts regarding life in an African country. A major fact that we learned was regarding vision screenings, which is the main aspect of our project. He said that living in the capital city, he never had an eye screening during his childhood. He also told us that many people never get their eye screened and many have vision problems, but didn’t focus on it. And in Genna Mvalo’s interview we learned of general health care in Malawi. A major point is that unlike here in the US there are not routine checkups. Only newborns have monthly routine checkups for their first year. After that people only visit the doctors if a problem comes up. Along with this, eye/vision screening is not routine process; it is seen as a secondary priority when compared to medicine and health care.

Solution:

After discussing for 2 weeks and using what we learned in class (Pugh Charts, Canvas, Radical thinking, etc.), we have decided on the mobile lens changer device as our solution. The mobile lens changer device will be a diagnostic device that helps determine prescription requirements. When worn, the devices will allow the patient to rotate through locally sourced recycled lenses and determine an appropriate prescription. The Pugh chart was a big factor in deciding our solution. We thought of the Pugh chart as the most accurate way of determining a solution. The scored the highest while also getting very few negatives. The mobile lens changer device also filled the roles stated in our value proposition canvas and the radical thinking chart. We also thought this product would be the most economically friendly and allow for easy user interaction.

Initial prototyping strategy:

For our look-alike model we plan to us the same materials which would be readily available in LRS. We have decided on cardboard as our main building material. We have chosen cardboard for our material instead of using 3ds printing or using materials such as metal or plastic because this is representative of material found in LRS. This would allow us to most accurately copy the materials available and training required to put together our solution. This allows for a hand on approach and puts us into the shoes of a person in LRS. Cardboard is also inexpensive, durable in a Sub-Saharan setting, and allows for the product to be easily fixed or rebuilt if a problem arises. To allow for easy assembly there will be a design in which you can trace on the cardboard to allow for accurate and precise cutting and step by step instructions to put the design together.

Comparison of your initial prototyping strategy versus eventual requirements for manufacturing:

Out initial prototyping strategy will be very similar to the final build. The primary difference will be that real glasses lenses will be used in the final product. But aside from that, the process for eventual manufacturing will be very similar. A design will be drawn on cardboard and will be easily followed and cut out. They will come with instructions for assembly along with the lenses.

We are in the process of contacting and setting up an additional interview using our feedback from the DR1 presentations. A fellow student suggested us his aunt and uncle who are both opticians.




Post III

19 February 2017

Written by Toby



Idea Creation Process:

On February 23rd, 2017, we met as a group to conduct an intensive idea finding session. After sharing the results of the individual research concerning specific eye diseases, we decided to conduct the two following idea creation processes:

1. Brainstorming

First, we made clear that there aren’t any stupid/bad ideas. It’s very important that everything which comes into each one’s mind gets mentioned. Then, everyone got around 5 minutes to think about ideas one his/her own before each other shared them. It was interesting that only a couple of ideas got mentioned twice, while most of the ideas were unique. While presenting, an intense discussion came up and we started combining the ideas – which wasn’t on purpose by the way. But in the end, it helped us a lot and we came to some first good ideas.

2. Radical Thinking

Next, we decided to conduct the radical thinking process. As we were meeting in Ingalls, we took benefit of the white boards and tried to sketch our thoughts on the wall (see attached photo).

As the first level/main problem we chose ‘Vision Impairment in Sub-Saharan Africa’. The next level was ‘Diagnosis’, ‘Surgery’, ‘Screening’ and ‘Glasses’ and it represented the main issues in the process of vision impairment we decided to face. On the next levels, we split up the issues into smaller problems.

After this breakdown, we tried to find solutions for the problems that we found most appealing (colored in green) and explained them to each other.

Idea Selection

After we successfully conducted the two idea creation processes, we made a list with all twelve ideas that came up. We decided to find the top five ideas by a democratic election: Everyone had five votes and we picked the five ideas that had the most votes.

Here is a short description of our top five ideas:

1. Day to day item screening
The idea is that the writing on day to day items may serve as a decision finder if there is a vision problem or not. For example, if you put a Coke bottle in your hand and extend your arm, you should still be able to read the writing if your eyes are fine. In addition, conducting this method may detect color blindness.

2. Mobile Lens Changer as App
The goal of this app is to substitute the Phoropter which measures an individual’s refractive error and determines his/her eyeglass prescription. The patient watches on the doctor’s smartphone and the doctor switches through different images which take different prescriptions into account. The patient says which images he can see and which ones he can’t see and by doing this the doctor can conclude roughly the patient’s prescription.

3. Mobile Lens Changer as Device
The “Mobile Lens Changer as a Device” will be a diagnostic device that helps determine prescription requirements. When worn, the device will all the patient to rotate through locally sourced recycled lenses and determine an appropriate prescription.

4. App Phone Mount
The app phone mount will be a device that will allow for easy analysis of patients using vision screeening apps in quick succession. It will be a headset with the ability to attach a phone to the front. When using the vision screening apps it will make for fast acquisition of screenings and diagnosis. Then will be easily removed and placed on the next patient with minimal adjustment needed.

5. Doctor Advice Hotline
This hotline should be made for health workers in rural areas. By using this hotline, they get access to help from specialists easily when they are faced to more difficult eye problems. This further source can help to simplify the process of referral and maybe even minimize the number of referrals.

Pugh Chart

Next step was finding and weighting criteria to analyze and evaluate the top five ideas. We decided that the most important criteria for a better eye screening in LRS are cost, accuracy and the amount of training needed. Further criteria, we wanted to involve in our decision process, were the need and amount for power, durability and time.

As a reference object, we picked the use of the Snellen Chart as a general screening process.

Everyone in our group conducted the evaluation on his/her own before we discussed our findings and found a result as a group.

The result of the Pugh Chart corresponded with our intention and we are glad to announce that we are going to develop a…

Mobile Lens Changer (as Device)!!!

Elevator Pitch:

We are dedicated engineering students focused on diminishing the problems of eye care screening in sub-Saharan Africa. 80% of all visual impairment can be prevented or cured (WHO), but in these settings there is a great lack of ophthalmologists to perform screenings. We are developing a device to give more doctors the ability to perform accurate eye screening. This will result in a more standardized method for doctors to perform eye screening and reach more patients. We are looking to be introduced to a optometrists and public health official to help us further understand the problem and how we can help.

Post I

12 February 2017

Written by Davy Huang



   This week, our group worked on the initial planning stages of our projected. We discussed the project background, need statements and stakeholders.

Project background info:

Low vision problems are a global issue that hinders learning in school, job employment, and quality of life. Many can go undiagnosed unless properly tested. Our goal is to provide reliable testing for all.

Need Statements:

Need Statement (LRS):
A way to diagnose low vision problems in children and adults in Sub-Saharan Africa during a 30 minute appointment in order to quickly provide them with corrective lenses.

Need Statement (HRS):
A way to diagnose low vision problems in children and adults in Sub-Saharan Africa during a 30 minute appointment.

StakeHolders:

1) Patients
 a. Children
 Do you have trouble see?
 How long is too long for a doctor’s appointment.
 b. Adults
 Do you have trouble see?
 How long is too long for a doctor’s appointment.
2) Doctors
 How many patients do you usually see on a daily basis?
 How much time do you usually get with each?
3) Health officials
 What regions are in the most need?
4) Manufacturers of medical devices
 How many glasses can you manufacture per day?
5) Teacher/Employers
 Do your students get tested for eye problems yearly?
 How many have access to glasses if diagnosed with a problem.
6) Investor
7) Optometrist
 How long diagnostics appointments usually take?
8) International Council of Ophthalmology
 What standards should we match/exceed?
